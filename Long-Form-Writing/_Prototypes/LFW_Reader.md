# <Reader name>

## Who they are

*One paragraph. Concrete. Not "a general reader" — a specific kind of person, with specific habits, that the writer can call to mind. Example: "Educated general reader; mid-career professional; not in the writer's field; reads non-fiction across history, sociology, economics. Subscribes to The Atlantic. Reads Beard, Harari, Scheidel. Reads roughly twenty non-fiction books a year and abandons one in three."*

## Background they bring

*What this reader already knows when they open the book. The basis on which curse-of-knowledge detection rests. Be specific about what's familiar and what isn't.*

## What they reward

*Moves that land well with this reader. Concrete examples — not "good writing" but "lead with the example, follow with the principle"; not "clear structure" but "a paragraph that tells me where I am in the argument."*

## What they punish

*Moves that lose this reader. Concrete and specific. The Skeptic punishes unsupported claims; the Impatient Generalist punishes long stretches without payoff; the Domain Expert punishes oversimplification that crosses into wrong.*

## Where they resist

*Places this reader is predisposed to push back. Not generic skepticism — specific terrain where this reader has priors, expertise, or sensitivities that will engage when the prose enters that territory.*

## What they're patient with vs. impatient with

*Pacing, density, formality, footnotes, anecdotes, abstraction. This reader's tolerance for each is the calibration knob that makes READER-SIMULATION sessions useful.*

## Notes

*Anything else the AI should know when reading-as-this-reader. The reader's emotional terrain, their political sensitivities, their professional history that informs how they read.*

---

> **For the AI conducting READER-SIMULATION:** when you read a target atom as this Reader, you internally maintain this Reader's background, patience, and resistance profile. You don't argue with the writer about whether the Reader is right; you report what this Reader experiences. Stay in the Reader's head; report what's there.
>
> If you find that the Reader resists nothing and rewards everything in a drafted section, the Reader is likely under-modeled (see failure mode F21). Surface this; don't suppress it.
